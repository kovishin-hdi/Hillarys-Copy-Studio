# The Hillarys Service Brand Playbook
## Six Moments, Three Spaces, One Promise

*A working document for the marketing function. Built from the 192-review audit. Designed to be operational, not aspirational. Print it. Mark it up. Argue with it. Then ship the parts you agree with.*

---

## The Premise

The 4.22-star average is not the brand. The brand is the lowest-quality moment in the customer's journey. Anjali — the muse we built from the reviews — does not remember whether the curtains were fitted in 14 or 17 days. She remembers whether the WhatsApp group went silent after she paid. That silence is the brand.

This document is not about logos, fonts, or campaign copy. Those decisions are downstream of this. This document is about the **six moments** the customer actually experiences and the **three spaces** the brand actually inhabits, and what we will choreograph in each.

The principle to hold in your head: **personality becomes choreography becomes inevitability.** Right now Hillarys is being saved by Surya, Praveen, Preetham, and Venkatesh as individuals. We will turn what they do well into a script the next forty hires can perform on day one.

---

## The Promise We Are Making

Before any tactic, the one-line brand promise this playbook is built to deliver:

> **"We handle this so you don't have to."**

That is what Anjali is buying. Not curtains. Not fabric. Handling. Every action below is judged by whether it makes that promise more true or less true.

If a proposed marketing campaign does not increase the customer's trust that we will handle this for them, it is decoration and we deprioritise it. If a proposed operational change makes the customer feel less handled, we kill it even if it saves money.

---

## The Six Moments

Each moment below has four parts: **what it is, what currently happens, what we will choreograph, and the success metric.** The marketing manager owns the choreography design and the brand artefacts. The operations team owns the execution. We meet weekly to look at the metric.

### Moment 1: The Bell Ring

**What it is.** The first three seconds when the consultant rings the customer's doorbell. The customer is forming a permanent first impression before a word is spoken.

**What currently happens.** The consultant arrives in personal clothing, carries fabric samples in a generic bag or folder, often runs late without proactive notice. There is no visual signal that this person is from Hillarys.

**What we will choreograph.**
- Branded polo shirt, ironed, with the consultant's name embroidered.
- Branded leather catalogue case, designed to look like a sommelier's wine carrier — heavy, beautiful, presented at chest height when the door opens.
- A specific opening line, scripted: *"Hi, I'm [name] from Hillarys. May I take my shoes off before I come in?"*
- A WhatsApp message sent exactly 15 minutes before arrival: *"On my way, will be there at 4:15. – [name]"*
- A handwritten card with the consultant's direct number, presented within the first two minutes.

**Success metric.** Survey question two days later: *"On a scale of 1–10, how confident did you feel about Hillarys within the first five minutes of meeting our consultant?"* Target: average above 8.5.

**Marketing manager actions.**
1. Spec and source the catalogue case (samples within 30 days).
2. Spec and order the embroidered polo shirts (two per consultant, name embroidered, within 45 days).
3. Design and print the consultant business cards (within 14 days).
4. Write and circulate the opening script (within 7 days).

---

### Moment 2: The Swatch Unrolling

**What it is.** The moment the consultant opens the catalogue case on the customer's dining table or sofa and the fabrics come out. The customer is judging whether this is a curated experience or a transaction.

**What currently happens.** Fabrics get pulled out of the bag in stacks. The dining table gets covered. There is no ritual.

**What we will choreograph.**
- A small Hillarys-branded canvas mat (45 × 30 cm) gets placed on the table first, before any fabric. It protects the table and signals that we care.
- Fabrics are presented in a fixed sequence: light filtering first, blackout second, sheers last. Three at a time, never more — too much choice creates anxiety, not delight.
- The consultant asks one specific question before showing fabric: *"Tell me what you don't want first — that helps me show you the right things faster."* This is a script-borrowed move from interior designers and it cuts consultation time in half.
- Each fabric gets one sentence of context — the weave, the light behaviour, the room it tends to suit. Not a sales pitch, a piece of information. The customer is being educated, not sold.

**Success metric.** Average time from consultant arrival to fabric selection: target 45–60 minutes (currently anecdotally 90+, which means the customer is exhausted by the time they decide). And: customer sentiment about the consultation, captured the same evening via a one-tap survey.

**Marketing manager actions.**
1. Source and brand the canvas presentation mat (within 30 days).
2. Build a one-page "fabric vocabulary" cheat sheet for consultants — how to describe each fabric in one sentence (within 21 days, in collaboration with the design team).
3. Run a half-day workshop with all consultants on the new presentation script (within 60 days).

---

### Moment 3: The Quote

**What it is.** The number is given. The customer has to make the most emotionally loaded decision in the journey: pay or don't.

**What currently happens.** A quote is given, often verbally. Additions get tacked on later. The "additional ₹25k after I paid the advance" complaint shows up six times in the negative reviews. This is currently a brand-killer moment.

**What we will choreograph.**
- A single printed quote, on Hillarys letterhead, handed to the customer at the end of the consultation. Not emailed later. Handed.
- The quote includes a line item for everything: fabric, lining, rod, brackets, installation, delivery, GST. The total at the bottom is the total. There is no further charge unless the customer changes the order.
- A line at the bottom of the quote, in italics: *"This is the final price. No additions, no surprises. If anything changes, we will tell you before we change it, and you can say no."*
- The consultant says, out loud, while handing it over: *"Take the next 24 hours with this. I'll WhatsApp you tomorrow evening to answer any questions, no pressure."*

**Success metric.** Percentage of orders where the final invoice equals the original quote: target 100%. Any deviation gets escalated to the marketing manager personally and root-caused weekly.

**Marketing manager actions.**
1. Redesign the quote document with the design team — must look beautiful, like a deliverable in itself, not a spreadsheet (within 30 days).
2. Work with finance to standardise the line items so every quote has the same structure (within 21 days).
3. Build the "no surprises" promise into the consultation script and the post-quote follow-up (within 30 days).

---

### Moment 4: The Silence After Advance

**What it is.** This is the single most damaging moment in the current Hillarys journey. The customer has paid 50% — typically ₹30,000 to ₹80,000. They are now anxious. They want to be told that we have them. Currently, they hear nothing for several days, sometimes weeks.

**What currently happens.** A WhatsApp group gets created. Often no one posts in it. The customer chases. Sometimes a CRM person replies, sometimes not. By the time installation happens, the customer has already lost trust.

**What we will choreograph.**
- Within 60 minutes of the advance being received, the consultant who closed the deal sends a personal WhatsApp message to the customer (not in a group): *"Thank you. I've passed your order to our production team. Pooja from coordination will WhatsApp you tomorrow morning by 11 with the production timeline. If you don't hear from her by 11, message me directly. — [name]"*
- This is the **Single Named Handover.** Not a group. Not a generic message. One named person handing the customer off to one named other person. The previous owner stays accessible for a week, by name, on WhatsApp.
- A confirmed WhatsApp message from coordination, the next morning, with three things: production start date, dispatch date, installation date. All three dates. In one message.
- A status update every Wednesday at 10am until installation is complete. Even if nothing has changed. Especially if nothing has changed. *"Wednesday update — your order is in stitching, on track for 14 February dispatch. Any questions, message me. – Pooja."*

**Success metric.** The "Wednesday Promise" — 100% of active orders receive a Wednesday status message. Tracked weekly. This is the single most important metric in the whole playbook. If we do nothing else from this document, do this.

**Marketing manager actions.**
1. Co-design the handover script with the operations head (within 14 days).
2. Build a weekly Wednesday update cadence into the CRM workflow with operations (within 30 days).
3. Create a simple Hillarys-branded WhatsApp message template library — twelve standard messages, each thoroughly word-tested, that coordinators can copy-paste (within 21 days).
4. Audit the WhatsApp groups weekly for two months. Any group with more than 24 hours of unanswered customer messages is escalated to the marketing manager and to operations.

### Moment 5: The Installer Arriving

**What it is.** The customer opens the door for the second time in the journey. The first time was the consultant; the customer formed an impression of "premium European brand." Now the test: does the second visit confirm or destroy that impression?

**What currently happens.** The installer often arrives in personal clothing. Sometimes from Porter. Sometimes a different person than promised. In several reviews, the installer himself admitted he was not employed by Hillarys. This destroys the brand instantly.

**What we will choreograph.**
- Every installer wears a Hillarys polo shirt with their name and a Hillarys ID card on a lanyard. Visible from the door.
- Every installer carries a Hillarys-branded toolkit — a real one, not a sticker on a generic bag.
- Every installer brings a Hillarys-branded drop cloth, large enough to cover the customer's furniture and floor under the work area. This gets laid down before any drilling.
- Every installer carries a printed installation card that lists what they will do today, in order. They show this to the customer at the door: *"Here's what I'll do today, in this order. Should take about two hours. Anything you want me to know before I start?"*
- If we ever have to use a third-party installer (which we should aim to phase out entirely): they wear our polo, carry our toolkit, and the customer is told nothing different. The day-labour admission gets banned in writing in the installer contract.

**Success metric.** Zero reviews in any quarter mentioning that the installer was not from Hillarys, was unprepared, or did not have proper tools. Currently this is in roughly 7 of the 33 negative reviews — about 21%. Target: 0% within two quarters.

**Marketing manager actions.**
1. Source and order the polo shirts, ID cards, lanyards, drop cloths, branded toolkits (within 60 days, ideally before the next big intake).
2. Design and print the installation cards — one per service type (curtains, blinds, motorised) — with the customer-facing checklist (within 30 days).
3. Work with operations to write the third-party installer brand standards contract clause (within 45 days).

---

### Moment 6: The Last Ten Feet

**What it is.** The final five minutes of the installation, before the installer leaves. The Ritz-Carlton principle — this is what gets remembered, not the check-in.

**What currently happens.** Installer packs up, sweeps loosely, leaves. Some are excellent (Surya, Praveen). Some are not. There is no consistent ritual.

**What we will choreograph.**
- The installer walks the customer through every window, opens and closes each curtain or blind once with them watching, demonstrates how to operate it.
- A Hillarys-branded care card gets placed on each window — a small, beautiful card the size of a business card, with the curtain's fabric name, care instructions, and a QR code linking to a one-minute video on cleaning and adjustment.
- The installer hands over a Hillarys folder containing: warranty certificate (printed, not emailed), care guide, the consultant's number, the coordinator's number, and a direct number for warranty claims.
- The installer takes a photo of every window with the customer's permission, sends it to the WhatsApp group (which now goes from sales channel to ongoing support channel).
- The closing line, scripted: *"Thank you for choosing Hillarys. If anything ever feels off — even a year from now — message Preetham or me directly. We'll handle it."*
- A WhatsApp message from the consultant who originally closed the deal, sent the next morning: *"Hope you're loving them. Five-year warranty starts today. Message me anytime."*

**Success metric.** Net Promoter Score, captured exactly seven days after installation. Target: 70+ within two quarters.

**Marketing manager actions.**
1. Design and produce the care cards, the warranty folder, the closing script (within 45 days).
2. Build the seven-day NPS survey via WhatsApp with operations (within 30 days).
3. Create the consultant follow-up template and ensure it is sent within 24 hours of installation, no exceptions (within 14 days).

## The Three Spaces

A space is a physical or digital container that the brand inhabits. The customer's emotional baseline is set by the space before any human interaction begins. Three spaces matter most. Two are physical, one is digital.

### Space 1: The Customer's Living Room During Consultation

This is the space we already get most right, and it is our biggest moat. Most furniture and curtain shopping in Bangalore happens in showrooms. Hillarys brings the showroom into the customer's home. This is genuinely differentiated. It must be defended.

What makes this space work: the customer is in their own territory, comfortable, with their partner, with the actual light and walls and furniture the curtains will live with. The consultant is, briefly, a guest in their world — which inverts the power dynamic of normal retail and creates trust faster.

What we have to design for in this space: **everything the consultant brings into the room is part of the brand.** The catalogue case, the fabric mat, the way fabric is handled, the laptop they put down to write the quote, the pen they hand the customer, the card they leave behind. All of it is brand. All of it is currently un-designed.

**Marketing manager owns:** sourcing, designing, and replenishing every physical artefact the consultant brings into the customer's home. Quarterly audit of what each consultant is actually carrying versus what they should be.

### Space 2: The Customer's Home on Installation Day

This is the space we currently get most wrong. The installer arrives and brings: themselves, their tools, the curtains. There is no brand presence beyond the curtain itself. Drilling happens. The customer's space gets dirtied. The Hillarys brand is briefly indistinguishable from a local contractor.

What we have to design for in this space: the brand has to be **visibly present at every stage**. The polo shirt at the door. The drop cloth on the floor. The toolkit on the side table. The branded packaging the curtain comes out of. The card placed on the window. The folder handed over at the end. A curious child or a visiting relative walking through the room during installation should see Hillarys, not a generic installation in progress.

**Marketing manager owns:** the visible brand kit that goes into every home with every installer. The packaging the curtain arrives in (this is your billboard inside the customer's bedroom for the first five minutes). The leave-behind artefacts that stay in the home permanently.

### Space 3: The WhatsApp Group Between Sale and Installation

This is a digital space we currently open and abandon. The customer enters this space anxious, has paid us a lot of money, and finds silence. This is where most of our brand damage happens.

What we have to design for in this space: **the silence is the enemy.** The WhatsApp group is a brand surface that is always-on, lives on the customer's most-used app, and updates them about the largest single home purchase they have made this quarter. It deserves the same design attention as the website, and currently gets none.

Specifically:
- The group naming convention should be standardised: *"Hillarys – [Customer Name] – Order #J0002230"*. Not generic.
- The group should have a Hillarys-designed display picture, not a default.
- The first message in the group, sent automatically on creation, should set expectations: *"Welcome! Your project owners here are: Pooja (coordination), Surya (installation), Preetham (overall). You'll hear from Pooja every Wednesday with an update. If you ever feel out of the loop, tag any of us and we'll respond within 4 working hours."*
- The Wednesday update is non-negotiable. It is the heartbeat of the space.
- When the order completes, the group does not get archived. It becomes the warranty channel. The customer keeps it on their phone forever as proof that we are reachable.

**Marketing manager owns:** the WhatsApp group brand standards document, the message templates library, the weekly audit of group health, and the escalation when groups go silent.

## What We Will Stop Doing

Equally important. Service brands die from bloat and inconsistency, not from too few campaigns.

- **Stop running performance marketing campaigns that promise speed.** "15 days" is currently in the website and being broken in 36% of negative reviews. Either change the promise to 21 days and hit it, or pause the campaign until 15 is a number we can actually deliver on. Better promise, kept, beats bigger promise, broken.
- **Stop creating WhatsApp groups we won't staff.** A group with no replies is worse than no group at all. If a coordinator cannot commit to the Wednesday update for a group, do not create that group — use one-to-one WhatsApp instead.
- **Stop calling ourselves a "premium London-based brand" in the sales conversation.** The customers repeat this phrase back to us in the worst reviews. It is doing more brand damage than brand good. Change the language to something we can actually live up to: *"50 years of European craftsmanship, now made in India."* True. Defensible. Doesn't set the customer up to feel cheated.
- **Stop sending installers without IDs.** This is non-negotiable from today.

## A Last Note

The reason this works — and the reason it is hard — is that almost none of it is glamorous. There is no campaign launch, no brand film, no influencer partnership. There is a polo shirt with a name on it, a card placed on a window, a WhatsApp message at 10am on a Wednesday. The brand is built in those small consistent acts, performed thousands of times, by people who cannot vary the script even on a bad day.

This is the discipline of building a service brand. It does not look like marketing. It looks like operations choreographed by marketing. The marketing manager's job, after this, is not to run campaigns. It is to be the keeper of the choreography, the auditor of the performance, and the person who walks into a customer's home unannounced once a month to see whether what we said we would do is what is actually happening.

If we get this right, the Google reviews will fix themselves. We will not need to ask for them. Anjali will write them, unprompted, three weeks after installation, on a Thursday night when the child is asleep — and she will write the 240-word version that names three people, but this time as heroes, and this time about a brand that handled it for her, exactly as promised.

That is the deliverable. The rest is just work.
